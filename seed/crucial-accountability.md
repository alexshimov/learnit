Q: When someone willfully breaks a promise, why does leading with power (threats, shame, force) backfire?
A: Raw power can move bodies but rarely hearts and minds — it buys short-term, bitter compliance. We default to it because of the fundamental attribution error: we read the behavior as a fixed character flaw and conclude force is the only fix.

::pull
Raw power moves bodies. It rarely moves **hearts and minds**.

---

Q: What are the three hidden costs of leading with power?
A: What force quietly takes from you:

::steps
**Kills relationships** — you shift from respected partner to feared enforcer; you can win the task and lose the person.
**Motivates resistance** — force signals distrust, destroys safety, and yields grudging compliance, never willing commitment.
**Doesn't last** — Lewin's 1930s study: authoritarian leadership got the highest output *while the leader was in the room*, the lowest once they left.

---

Q: What does the "Law of the Hog" (the plywood mill) teach about power?
A: Everyone has *some* power. Abuse your power over someone with less and, somehow, a "hog gets fed" — sabotage, endless complaining, or quietly letting problems grow. All of it expensive.

::takeaway The power check
Before you speak, ask: "When I'm done with this conversation, will they use their power **for** me or **against** me?"

---

Q: Why is it wrong to say someone "isn't motivated"?
A: Everyone is always motivated by *something* — the only question is by what. When we choose what to do, we look ahead and ask "what will this get me?", then pick whatever yields the best **consequence bundle**.

::pull
Change their view of the **consequence bundle**, and their behavior follows.

---

Q: What is the difference between natural and imposed consequences?
A: Reach for the natural kind first.

::compare
Imposed | Punishments or incentives you add on to change behavior — they need your authority. Effective short-term, but they pull strings and breed resistance. | bad
Natural | The outcomes already attached to the behavior — no power needed. Always present, and what accountability experts reach for first. | good

---

Q: A natural consequence doesn't always motivate. What's the catch?
A: It only motivates if the person *cares* about it. ("Mom: when you cut Liam off, it hurts his feelings." "Son: good, I don't like him anyway.") The job isn't to list any consequence — it's to find the ones connected to what they **already** care about.

---

Q: When looking for consequences that matter to someone, which three motivation sources should you sweep?
A: Personal, social, and structural — each asks a different question:

::cascade
Personal (S1) | how it affects them
Social (S3) | how it affects others they care about
Structural (S5) | rewards & punishments already in the system

---

Q: What question does the personal motivation source (S1) ask, and how do you use it?
A: **"How will this affect them in a way they personally care about?"**

::steps
**Link to existing values** — "Why is this so important to you?" (→ "I want to be there for my kid's soccer seasons.")
**Tie short-term ease to long-term pain** — "Double-checking the calendar is a hassle, but I worry your reputation will suffer if we can't solve this."
**Focus on the long-term benefit** — "You want the director role. Present every other month and you'll build the confidence within a year."

---

Q: What question does the social motivation source (S3) ask, and how do you use it?
A: **"How will this affect other people in a way the person cares about?"**

::steps
**Introduce the hidden victims** — "When you show up late, the rest of us carry what you didn't."
**Hold up a mirror** — "You may see it as persuasive, but when you raise your voice, the team starts seeing it as overbearing."

---

Q: What question does the structural motivation source (S5) ask — and the trap to avoid?
A: **"How do their actions naturally lead to rewards or punishments already in the system?"** Surface the ones already there — "people who meet every deadline get promoted faster."

::warn Don't invent new ones
Inventing fresh rewards or threats turns natural motivation into imposed power. Find what the current context already rewards.

---

Q: What does "make the invisible visible" mean, and why not just repeat consequences people already know?
A: Real motivation comes from surfacing the consequences they care about but *can't see* — because those outcomes happen out of sight or far down the line. Repeating the known just sounds patronizing.

---

Q: In the Eulogio (lipstick mirrors) story, what actually made the kiss-prints stop?
A: He dipped his squeegee in the *toilet* and wiped a mirror with it — revealing a natural consequence the girls had never seen. No threat; he made a new connection in their brains.

---

Q: How do you tell a threat apart from revealing a natural consequence?
A: It comes down to intent.

::compare
A threat | Intent to punish; you take pleasure in describing the awful "or else." | bad
A natural consequence | Intent to solve the problem for both of you; you reveal something real so they can choose with open eyes. | good

---

Q: When you make the invisible visible, what two cautions matter?
A: Keep it safe, and don't overdo it.

::warn Two cautions
If they hear a threat anyway (maybe a past boss burned them), step out and restore safety — explain your positive intent. And stop when you reach compliance: once you believe they'll act, quit explaining. Don't sell past the close.

---

Q: When should you shift from natural to imposed consequences (discipline)?
A: When the behavior needs immediate action, or you've already had the content, pattern, and relationship conversations and nothing has changed. Then move to imposed consequences — carefully.

---

Q: What are the four rules for using discipline well?
A: Discipline isn't imposed with pleasure — follow the mechanics.

::steps
**Know the mechanics** — follow your org's disciplinary steps, or your action gets thrown out on review.
**Partner with authority** — check the right steps with specialists (usually HR) before acting.
**Be appropriately somber** — serious tone; talk about what *must* be done, not what you *get* to do.
**Be consistent** — never discipline to get even; reviewers examine equity first.

---

Q: In the "how to save a life" story (Cody & Diego), what was the most potent move?
A: Diego helped Cody see the consequences he already faced but wasn't considering — the mom who won't take him back, the shelter bed he'd miss by 5 p.m. "Don't decide on impulse — spend the night." Even at the edge, revealing unseen consequences beat force.

---

Q: Summarize Chapter 8 ("Make It Motivating") in three moves.
A: The whole chapter in order:

::steps
You don't motivate people — consequences do; people are already driven by what they anticipate.
Connect with natural consequences first; only impose when necessary.
Make the invisible visible — help them see what happens if they stay the same or change.
